◆ Track 6 · Experimentation & A/B Testing

Analyzing & Interpreting Results

p-values done right, confidence intervals, and decisions.

20 min readBeginner-friendlyLesson 4 of 7 in track
◎ Why this matters

The test finished and B is up 1.1%. Ship it? Not so fast. Reading an experiment correctly is where careful analysts earn their keep: separating a real effect from noise, a statistically significant result from a practically meaningful one, and resisting the urge to slice the data until something looks good. This lesson turns raw counts into a decision you can defend — and it's the analysis half of every A/B-test interview question.

ConceptThe two-proportion test

For a conversion-rate test you have four numbers: users and conversions in each arm. The analysis asks: is the gap between the two rates bigger than you'd expect from chance alone? That's a two-proportion z-test — the same hypothesis-testing logic from Lesson 4.8, applied to two rates. You compute the difference, its standard error, turn that into a z-score and a p-value, and — most importantly — a confidence interval on the lift itself:

analyze_ab.py
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# observed results of the experiment
nA, xA = 12000, 1140   # control:   12,000 users, 1,140 converted
nB, xB = 12000, 1272   # treatment: 12,000 users, 1,272 converted
pA, pB = xA/nA, xB/nB
diff = pB - pA

# p-value: pooled standard error (assumes the null — no difference)
pool = (xA + xB) / (nA + nB)
se_test = np.sqrt(pool*(1-pool) * (1/nA + 1/nB))
z = diff / se_test
pval = 2 * (1 - stats.norm.cdf(abs(z)))

# confidence interval on the lift: unpooled standard error
se_ci = np.sqrt(pA*(1-pA)/nA + pB*(1-pB)/nB)
lo, hi = diff - 1.96*se_ci, diff + 1.96*se_ci

print(f"control {pA:.1%}   treatment {pB:.1%}   lift {diff:+.1%}")
print(f"p-value: {pval:.4f}   ->  {'significant' if pval < 0.05 else 'not significant'} at 0.05")
print(f"95% CI on the lift: [{lo:+.1%}, {hi:+.1%}]")
Output
control 9.5%   treatment 10.6%   lift +1.1%
p-value: 0.0046   ->  significant at 0.05
95% CI on the lift: [+0.3%, +1.9%]

A +1.1% lift, p = 0.005 (unlikely to be chance), and a 95% CI of roughly [+0.3%, +1.9%] that excludes zero — a real, positive effect. Notice we report the interval, not just the point estimate: the truth is plausibly anywhere in that range, and the width tells you how precise your estimate is.

Concept · report this, not just pThe confidence interval is the real answer

A p-value alone throws away the most useful information — how big is the effect and how sure are we? The confidence interval on the lift keeps both. Three readings: CI entirely above 0 → a real positive effect (ship, if it clears your bar); CI straddles 0 → inconclusive (you couldn't distinguish it from no effect); CI very wide → imprecise, likely underpowered, consider running longer. Report the interval, and the decision usually makes itself.

Concept · the trap of huge NStatistical vs practical significance

✗ Common pitfall

With a big enough sample, even a trivial effect becomes statistically significant — because the standard error shrinks toward zero, a +0.01% lift can hit p < 0.05. But a 0.01% lift may not be worth the engineering cost, the added complexity, or the guardrail risk. Statistical significance says "the effect is probably real"; practical significance asks "is it big enough to matter?" Always compare the confidence interval against a pre-set practical threshold (the MDE from Lesson 6.3). A result can be significant and useless, or non-significant yet promising — the p-value can't tell those apart, but the effect-size interval can.

ConceptDon't torture the data: multiple testing

The other way to fool yourself is to test many things and celebrate whatever crossed 0.05. Check 20 metrics, or slice by 10 user segments, and at α = 0.05 you expect about one false positive by pure chance — the multiple comparisons problem. Defences: decide your one primary metric and any segments before the test (pre-registration); and if you must test many, correct the threshold (e.g. Bonferroni: divide α by the number of tests). "We dug around and found a significant lift in left-handed users in Canada" is almost always noise, not a discovery.

Your result
Hint. Rates pA=xA/nA, pB=xB/nB; pooled pool=(xA+xB)/(nA+nB); se=np.sqrt(pool*(1-pool)*(1/nA+1/nB)); z=(pB-pA)/se; p=2*(1-stats.norm.cdf(abs(z))).
Reference solution.
pA, pB = xA/nA, xB/nB
pool = (xA + xB) / (nA + nB)
se = np.sqrt(pool*(1-pool) * (1/nA + 1/nB))
z = (pB - pA) / se
answer = round(float(2 * (1 - stats.norm.cdf(abs(z)))), 4)
8% vs 9% on 8,000 users each gives a small p-value — a real, if modest, lift. Report the confidence interval too, and check the lift clears your practical bar before shipping.


★ Key points to remember
  • Analyze a conversion test with a two-proportion z-test: difference in rates ÷ its standard error → z → p-value.
  • Report the confidence interval on the lift, not just p: CI above 0 = real effect; CI straddling 0 = inconclusive; wide CI = imprecise.
  • Statistical significance (probably real) ≠ practical significance (big enough to matter) — with huge N, trivial effects go significant. Compare the CI to your practical threshold.
  • Multiple comparisons: test 20 metrics/segments and 1 crosses 0.05 by chance. Pre-register the primary metric; correct (Bonferroni~) if testing many.
  • A result can be significant and useless, or non-significant yet promising — the effect-size interval, not the p-value, tells them apart.

◆ Practice▸
1An experiment reports: lift +0.4%, p = 0.21, 95% CI on the lift = [−0.2%, +1.0%]. Write the one-paragraph readout you'd send your PM.
Show solution & reasoning
"The test is inconclusive. B is nominally +0.4% but that's not statistically significant (p = 0.21), and the 95% confidence interval — [−0.2%, +1.0%] — includes zero, so we can't rule out no effect (or even a small negative one). The interval is also fairly wide, which suggests we're underpowered: we simply don't have enough data to tell a small win from nothing. Recommendation: either run longer to tighten the interval, or accept that any real effect is likely small (≤ ~1%) and decide whether that's worth shipping on other grounds." No overclaiming, effect size front and centre.
2Explain why a +5% lift with p = 0.30 and a +0.1% lift with p = 0.001 can both be the wrong thing to ship, for opposite reasons.
Show solution & reasoning
The +5%, p = 0.30 result is practically large but not statistically reliable — it might be a noisy fluke (probably underpowered), so shipping risks chasing a mirage; you'd run longer to confirm. The +0.1%, p = 0.001 result is statistically rock-solid but practically trivial — a 0.1% gain may not justify the cost or complexity, so shipping spends effort on nothing. One fails statistical significance, the other fails practical significance; a good decision needs both — a real and meaningful effect.


◎ Deep dive: pooled vs unpooled SE, one- vs two-sided & sequential, and trustworthiness checks▸
Why two different standard errors. For the p-value you assume the null is true (no difference), so you estimate one shared conversion rate and use a pooled standard error. For the confidence interval you're estimating the actual difference, so you use each group's own rate — an unpooled standard error. They're usually close, but using the right one for each purpose is the mark of someone who understands the machinery rather than copying a formula. For ratio or per-user metrics (revenue per user), the variance is trickier and needs the delta method or bootstrapping.
One-sided vs two-sided, and the sequential temptation. Use a two-sided test by default: a change can hurt as easily as help, and you want to detect a regression too. A one-sided test doubles your effective false-positive rate for the direction you favour — a subtle form of p-hacking. And resist analysing early and often on a fixed-horizon test; if you truly need to peek and stop early, use methods designed for it (sequential testing, alpha-spending, or Bayesian approaches), which control the error rate under repeated looks.
Trustworthiness before conclusions. Before believing any effect, seasoned experimenters run a battery of sanity checks: the SRM (Lesson 6.2), a pre-period A/A test (split identically with no change — you should see no effect; if you do, your pipeline is biased), and checks that the effect is stable over time and consistent across major segments (a huge effect in one tiny segment driving the whole result is a red flag). Twyman's law: any figure that looks interesting or surprising is probably wrong — verify the plumbing before you trust the number.


◆ Interview-ready
Show judgement, not just a p-value: analyze with a two-proportion test, but report the confidence interval on the lift; separate statistical significance (probably real) from practical significance (big enough to matter — huge N makes trivial effects significant); and call out multiple comparisons when someone slices until something crosses 0.05. Bonus: A/A tests and SRM as trust checks.
