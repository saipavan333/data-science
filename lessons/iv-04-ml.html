◆ Track 14 · Interview Bank

Machine Learning Bank

From bias–variance to deployment.

20 min readBeginner-friendlyLesson 4 of 6 in track
◎ Why this matters

The ML round tests judgement, not whether you can call .fit(). Interviewers want to hear you reason about the bias–variance tradeoff, defend a metric choice, catch leakage, and design a model end to end. The through-line of every strong answer: models are easy to run and easy to fool yourself with — show that you know the difference.

Concept + judgementThe questions that recur

◆ The ML deep-dive drill

Before moving on, make sure you could answer these out loud:

  • Explain the bias–variance tradeoff, with a lever for each side.
  • How do you prevent overfitting? (more data, regularization, CV, simpler model, early stopping)
  • What does regularization (L1/L2) do — and how do they differ?
  • Precision vs recall — and when do you optimise each?
  • What does ROC-AUC measure, and when do you prefer PR-AUC?
  • Bagging vs boosting — what does each reduce (variance vs bias)?
  • How do random forests and gradient boosting actually work?
  • What is data leakage, and how do you prevent it?
  • How do you handle class imbalance?
  • "The model is 99% accurate" — what do you ask before believing it?
  • Walk me through building and validating a model end to end.

The concept behind most othersWorked answer — bias–variance

"Total error splits into bias (error from wrong assumptions — too simple a model, underfitting), variance (error from sensitivity to the training sample — too complex, overfitting), and irreducible noise. A model too simple has high bias and misses real patterns; too complex has high variance and memorises noise. The tradeoff is that reducing one often raises the other, and the goal is the sweet spot that minimises error on unseen data." Then name levers: increase flexibility (more features, deeper trees) to cut bias; decrease it (regularization, pruning, more data, ensembling) to cut variance. Connecting the levers to the concept is what earns the point.

Explain the mechanismWorked answer — how gradient boosting works

"Gradient boosting builds trees sequentially, each one correcting the errors of the ensemble so far. You start with a weak prediction, compute the residuals (what you got wrong), fit a small tree to those residuals, add it (scaled by a learning rate), and repeat. Because each tree attacks the current mistakes, boosting mainly reduces bias, building a strong model from many weak ones." Contrast with bagging / random forests, which build many independent trees in parallel on bootstrapped samples and average them to reduce variance. The crisp "sequential-fixes-bias vs parallel-averages-variance" distinction is the senior tell.

★ Key points to remember

  • The ML round tests judgement: metric choice, leakage, validation, and the bias–variance tradeoff — not .fit() syntax.
  • Underfitting = high bias (too simple); overfitting = high variance (too complex). Levers: add flexibility to cut bias; regularize/ensemble/more data to cut variance.
  • Bagging (random forests) averages independent trees → ↓ variance; boosting adds sequential error-correcting trees → ↓ bias.
  • On imbalanced data, reject accuracy — use recall/precision/PR-AUC and compare to the majority baseline.
  • For "how would you build X," give the end-to-end workflow: frame → leakage-safe features → split → baseline → model → honest metrics → interpret.

◆ Practice▸
1"How would you build a model to detect fraudulent transactions?" Give a structured, end-to-end answer.
Show solution & reasoning

Clarify first: what's the fraud rate (likely <1% — severe imbalance), the cost of a missed fraud vs. a false alarm, and latency needs (real-time?). Frame: binary classification, but recall-heavy — missing fraud is costly. Data/features (leakage-safe): only signals known at transaction time — amount, velocity (transactions/hour), deviation from the user's norm, geo/device mismatch, time-of-day; drop anything populated after a fraud label. Split by time (train past, test future) to avoid temporal leakage. Handle imbalance: class weights / resampling, and evaluate on PR-AUC, recall, and precision@k — never accuracy. Model: start with a regularized logistic baseline, then gradient-boosted trees; wrap preprocessing in a Pipeline. Threshold: tune to the cost of each error (likely low threshold for high recall, with a human-review queue for flagged cases). Interpret with SHAP to confirm sensible drivers and catch leakage, monitor for drift (fraud is adversarial and shifts), and A/B test the intervention. The interviewer is listening for: clarifying questions, leakage/imbalance awareness, the right metrics, and end-to-end thinking — not a fancy model.
